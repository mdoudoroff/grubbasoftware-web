TrueGrain 2 Help

This document provides a concise operational reference to TrueGrain. It does not repeat contextual detail found on the TrueGrain web site. If neither this document nor the web site adequately addresses your questions, please don’t hesitate to contact us.

Opening source files

TrueGrain can load:

  • color or grayscale uncompressed TIF files with 8-bits or 16-bits per channel
  • JPEG images

TrueGrain will honor whatever ICC profile is embedded in the source image. There is no need to deviate from whatever working space you have adopted in Photoshop or Lightroom.

Generally speaking, the best source material for TrueGrain is a high resolution 16-bit color TIF (or grayscale if you’re bypassing TrueGrain’s spectral and dynamic range conversion features). However, the absolute highest resolution possible source photo is not always what you want:

In terms of spatial resolution, the amount of detail in your source image needs to have some reasonable relationship with the grain you will be applying in TrueGrain. With today’s digital cameras, it’s not that hard to produce an image with more detail than a film camera of comparable frame size could have ever produced. Given the grain samples in TrueGrain were scanned at 4000 dpi, the following are the theoretical maximum resoulutions you would want your source imagery to be, depending on the film size you wish to simulate:

1102362 x 1732
1355354 x 3465
6x4.57874 x 6250
6x67874 x 7874
6x811340 x 7874

Don’t get too hung up on all this trivia, however: start with your full resolution source image and find the look you desire in TrueGrain. If you’re using for really large grain structure and you feel you’re seeing too much image detail amidst the grain, try scaling a copy of your source image down to the appropriate size (see table, above) and process that with the same settings.

Choosing a film

After loading an image, your next step will be to choose a film stock from the Film Type selector.

If film stocks aren’t available in the selector, click the button next to it with the gear icon, and download one or more films.

After you choose a film, the preview image will update to show your image converted to the selected film with default settings.

Saving your converted image

To save your converted image on top of the file you opened, choose Save from the File menu.

To save your converted image to a new file, choose Save as... from the File menu.

In any case, your processed image will be saved as a 16-bit monochrome TIF file with an embedded Gray Gamma 2.2 ICC profile.

Adjusting the preview

You can adjust the preview window with the Preview controls:

If you disable the Preview with the switch, the preview will no longer render—you will be flying blind. However, this switch is useful to toggle back and forth and compare the processed results with your original image.

The select box control enables you to zoom in or out on your image. While zoomed in, you can pan about the image by clicking and dragging in the preview display.

Customizing how the film grain is applied

To customize how film grain is applied to your image, choose the Film Grain tab, and make sure it isn’t disabled with the switch.

Changes you make in the Film Grain settings are reflected in the preview immediately after you make them.

To select the size (scale) of the grain, make a different choice in the Film Size Presets or enter your own scaling percentage with the slider. (You will probably want to zoom to 100% in the preview window when doing this, so that you can really see the grain.) Scale is relative to the largest size of film grain provided. A graphic representation of your source image is provided against the currently-selected grain sample to provide a sense of how your scale choice would relate to an actual piece of film.

Two controls are provided to adjust the intensity of the applied grain:

The slider will simply make the grain more or less intense (coarse), with the middle position corresponding to the neutral, measured intensity.

The editable curve controls how strongly the grain expresses itself across the dynamic range of the image (dark to light). By default, this curve represents the neutral, measured behavior of the film, but you can adjust the curve for creative purposes. (Right-click on the curve for more options.)

Customizing the grayscale conversion

TrueGrain converts color images to grayscale in a way that gets very close to how a given film stock responds to both the quantity and wavelength (color) of light. The more saturated tones of your source image are governed by the film’s spectral response profile. The more neutral tones of your source image are governed by the film’s dynamic range profile. You have custom control over these profiles in the Spectral Response and Dynamic Range tags of the interface.

Spectral Response controls

Once you choose a film, the curve in this tab is updated to show the spectral response to hue for that film. This curve is derived from a measured profile.

Additional profiles—color filters commonly used in black & white photography—for the selected film can be chosen from the Filter selector.

The curve in the above example suggests that the currently-selected film is more sensitive to blues and less sensitive to greens. Consequently, the blues in your image will appear lighter and the greens, darker.

For custom effects, you can edit the curve simply by clicking and dragging on the nodes. Moreover, if you right-click (ctrl+click on a MacBook) on the curve, you can add or remove nodes, flatten or flip the curve, or restore it to its defaults.

The best way to understand the spectral response controls is to play around with them. A picture with strong colors will help.

 

Dynamic Range controls

The Dynamic Range curve reflects a measured profile for the currently-selected film type. This profile reflects the response characteristics of the film to neutral tones, from dark to light, and the curve will generally be vaguely shaped like an “s”, as above. If you are familiar with the “curves” control in Photoshop, you will find this tool similar.

For custom effects, you can edit the curve simply by clicking and dragging on the nodes. Moreover, if you right-click (ctrl+click on a MacBook) on the curve, you can add or remove nodes or flatten the curve. The opacity slider can be used to roll off the effects of the profile entirely. (Normally, opacity should be at 100%.)

In practice, you are most likely to make subtle changes to this curve—making it more or less steep—to alter the contrast of the image. Remember that the effects of this curve will be most clearly seen in the neutral (less saturated) areas of your image.
